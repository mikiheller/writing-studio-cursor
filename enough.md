# Enough

The bed knew before Madison did.

At 6:47 a.m., the array of piezoelectric sensors woven through the mattress registered the shift in her breathing, the faint deceleration of her heart rate that marked the end of her final REM cycle. The bed began its gentle undulation — a motion engineered to mimic the rocking of a sailboat on still water — easing her upward through the layers of sleep like a hand lifting something fragile to the surface. The temperature of the sheets rose two degrees. The blackout glass in the bedroom windows began its slow fade to translucent, letting in a careful pour of morning light.

Madison Bellcourt opened her eyes.

She lay there for a moment, cocooned in the silk-cashmere duvet — a blend developed by a materials lab in Zurich that somehow managed to feel like being held — and then swung her legs to the heated hardwood floor, which met her bare feet at precisely ninety-two degrees Fahrenheit. Her registered preference.

In the bathroom, she settled onto the toilet — a sixteen-thousand-dollar unit that most visitors mistook for a piece of minimalist sculpture. As she sat, the bowl's internal sensors went to work: spectrophotometric urinalysis, pH balance, cortisol levels, hydration index, micronutrient concentrations. Within ninety seconds, a full report had been compiled, encrypted, and transmitted to her nutritionist. By the time Madison finished brushing her teeth, the day's meals had already been recalibrated.

The house had been thinking about her all night. The climate system — fed by real-time weather data, barometric readings, and her own biometric baselines — held every room at 68.5 degrees with 42 percent humidity. In the kitchen, breakfast waited on the white quartz island: a bowl of warm ancient grains topped with slow-poached eggs from a heritage farm upstate, a tumble of microgreens, avocado mousse, and a long drizzle of high-polyphenol olive oil. Beside it, a small glass of tart cherry juice — selected to address the slightly elevated inflammation markers from last night's sleep data. It was, as it always was, extraordinary. She could taste how precisely it had been made for her, and for no one else.

She ate slowly, watching the morning light stretch across the room, and tapped the countertop display to check her accounts. The number was there, steady and familiar: **$98,412,637.18**. She looked at it for a moment. Then she closed the app and finished her juice.

Before heading out, she stepped into her jacket. It had been custom-tailored three months ago from a full-body geometry scan — cut from a graphene-threaded microfiber that weighed less than cotton but could blunt a January wind without flinching. Woven into the lining was a lattice of thermoelectric filaments that held her skin temperature within half a degree of comfort no matter what waited outside. The fabric was soft enough to sleep in, tough enough to last a decade. She zipped it to her chin and it fit the way only something made exclusively for your body can fit.

The car was already waiting in the driveway. A low, silent electric sedan — no steering wheel, no pedals, just a cabin that smelled faintly of cedar and opened its door the moment it recognized her gait. Inside, the seat adjusted to her lumbar profile before she'd finished sitting down. The windows tinted against the glare. A curated playlist began — ambient piano, assembled by an algorithm that had been studying her mood patterns for three years and had gotten very good at its job.

"Downtown," she said.

The car pulled from the curb without a sound.

Twenty-two minutes later, it eased to a stop on a blocked-off street thick with people. Madison stepped out, opened the trunk, and retrieved the sign she'd painted by hand the night before.

She raised it above her head and walked into the crowd.

**NO MORE TRILLIONAIRES.**

Madison held the sign high, her jaw set with the quiet, righteous fury of a woman who believed — truly, deeply believed — that she had not been given enough.
